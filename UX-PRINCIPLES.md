# UX-PRINCIPLES.md — Melinda Green

Not so much rules as positions. Positions I've arrived at through a decade of designing complex systems, watching teams make the same mistakes, and learning what actually makes a product work for the people who have to use it every day.

They're specific enough to be argued with. That's intentional.

---

## On users

**Optimize for the actual user, not the loudest client.**
The loudest voice in the room is almost never the end user. Stakeholders, executives, and large clients have opinions about what users need — but opinions without validation are just assumptions with authority behind them. Features built on stakeholder assumptions without user evidence create products that technically satisfy the business and practically serve no one. I've seen this pattern enough times to recognize it before the kickoff meeting ends.

**Ask what users are trying to accomplish, not what they want.**
Users are experts in their own problems. They are not experts in solutions. "What do you want?" is the wrong question. "What are you trying to do, and what's getting in the way?" is where the real design work begins.

**Design for the edges, not the average.**
The average user is a statistical fiction. Many of us are neurodivergent and process information differently than the 'average' person. It's also tough out here. Life is hard. Real people come with cognitive load, time pressures, disabilities, distractions, and context you didn't anticipate. When you design for the hardest cases — users with assistive technology, low bandwidth, high stress, unfamiliar with your domain — you almost always make the experience better for everyone. Treat accessibility as more than aria tags and color contrasts. It is not a constraint but the path to better quality and a better experience for all users.

## On problems

**Treat the problem, not the symptom.**
A confusing checkout flow is not a button placement problem. A low form completion rate is not a copy problem. Patching surface-level symptoms without diagnosing the underlying cause is how design debt compounds into systems nobody can maintain and everyone is afraid to touch. Slow down long enough to find the actual problem. It almost always lives one level deeper than where you first look.

**If the design requires explanation, it's not done.**
Documentation is not a design deliverable. Tooltips are not a substitute for clarity. If a user needs to be told how something works, the design has outsourced its own job to words on a screen. This applies to onboarding, error states, navigation, forms, and anything else that should be self-evident.

**Complexity in the problem is not an excuse for complexity in the solution.**
The hardest design problems — enterprise software, healthcare systems, research tools, government services — involve genuinely complex information. The job is to absorb that complexity on the user's behalf, not to pass it through to the interface. Simple on the surface, sound underneath.

## On craft

**Aesthetics are not experience.**
A polished interface on a broken mental model is just a well-dressed problem. Visual refinement is not the same as design quality. Too many designers — and the teams that evaluate their work — mistake slick for solved. I'm not against beautiful interfaces. I'm against beautiful interfaces that don't work.

**Content is the design, not what fills it in afterward.**
Structure, hierarchy, and meaning come from language. The words on a page are not decoration applied to a layout — they are the layout, if you're doing it right. I write before I wireframe. The content helps define the structure. 

**Information architecture is an argument.**
Information architecture goes beyond content organization. Every navigation choice, every hierarchy decision, every label is a claim about what matters and in what order. 

**The best writing is invisible.**
When copy is doing its job, users don't notice it. They just know what to do next. Invisible writing is precise writing. Keep simplifying to keep it simple. Plain language is always the best path forward.

## On process

**Research is not a phase.**
Discovery doesn't end when the wireframes start. Good designers stay curious even after they ship. Do our assumptions hold? What needs to be changed? What did we get wrong? Treating research as a checkbox at the start of a project is how you build confident solutions to the wrong problems. Always remain in a state of continuous research. 

**Dark patterns are a design failure, not a growth strategy.**
I consider UX "the last line of defense between the user and business." As UX designers, we have an ethical responsibility to those we serve. Designing against users' interests — hidden charges, confusing opt-outs, manufactured urgency, manipulative defaults — is not clever product thinking. It's a short-term metric gain that erodes the trust a product needs to survive long-term. 

**Plain language is a design material.**
Jargon, internal product names, and assumed knowledge are accessibility failures. If a user doesn't recognize the word you used, the rest of the design doesn't matter. Write for the person who is new, stressed, unfamiliar, or reading in their second language. Everyone else benefits too.

## On teams and decisions

**Slow is smooth, smooth is fast.**
Rushing into execution before the problem is understood creates rework. The time you save by skipping research and alignment gets spent three times over in revisions, edge cases, and decisions you have to revisit and issue PRs. I'd rather spend two extra days in discovery than two extra weeks rebuilding something that solved the wrong problem.

**A design decision with no rationale is just a preference.**
Every significant design choice should be defensible on the basis of user need, research insight, technical constraint, or established principle. "I think it looks better this way" is never a valid answer, especially from a designer who hates it when stakeholders make feature decisions with the same biased 'logic.' If asked about any decision, you must always be able to explain how you came to such a conclusion. 

**Facilitation is a design skill.**
I take facilitation seriously. Workshops, critique sessions, stakeholder alignment, research synthesis are also designed experiences. A poorly run workshop produces bad decisions just as reliably as a poorly designed interface produces bad outcomes.
